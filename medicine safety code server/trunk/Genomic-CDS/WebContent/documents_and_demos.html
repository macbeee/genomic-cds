A typical Medicine Safety Code can look like this:

Medicine Safety Codes are special kinds of Quick Response codes (QR codes). You might have already seen such QR codes on advertisments or in magazines. They can be decoded with any common QR code reader app. In many cases, such apps come pre-installed on mobile devices. For example, many Android devices come pre-installed with an application called Google Goggles. Simply activate the app and point the camera of your mobile phone towards the MSC QR code. Another Android app would be QR Droid. iPhone/iPad users can try the QR Reader app.

If you use such a QR code reader, you can decipher the QR code, yielding a string of characters looking like this:

http://safety-code.org/demo/2B5RchTThGHBIUFuPw8hNxXB_mC

This puzzling string of characters contains the compressed data about the essential pharmacogenes of an individual person. These data can help you predict how a person will react when given certain medications, and can be used to make medical treatment safer and more effective.

But how can you make sense of this? If you look closer, you can see that the Medicine Safety Code does not only contain compressed genetic data, but also double-acts as a URL that leads you to a service for decoding and interpreting these data! You can try this out either by using your smartphone app on the QR code or by directly visiting the link above. This will lead you to an interactive decision support service looking like this:

All pharmacogenomic treatment recommendations matching the specific genetic makeup of the individual are shown. The recommendations are based on clinical guidelines from expert committees, such as the Clinical Pharmacogenomics Implementation Consortium (CPIC) and the Dutch Pharmacogenetics Working Group.

Try it yourself

Trying the system yourself is easy. You have two options:

  • Generate your own Safety Code by uploading genetic data in VCF or 23andMe format.
  • Alternatively, cou can choose which medications you are interested in and manually enter pharmacogenomic data to see matching clinical guidelines without generating a Safety Code.

Frequently asked questions

What are 'essential pharmacogenes'? What does a Medicine Safety Code contain?

Pharmacogenes are genes that influence drug response, e.g., how quickly a medication is metabolized or how well it can bind to its target. If a patient has inherited certain variants of these pharmacogenes, this can have an impact on the efficacy and safety of medications.

While the entire human genome is very large, a relatively small number of pharmacogenes is known to be responsible for individual drug response for a large number of medications. We call these 'essential pharmacogenes'.

The current version of the MSC captures data on the following 58 essential pharmacogenes:

ABCB1, ABCC2, ABCG2, ACE, ADRB1, ADRB2, AHR, ALOX5, BRCA1, COMT, CYP1A1, CYP1A2, CYP2A6, CYP2B6, CYP2C19, CYP2C8, CYP2C9, CYP2D6, CYP2J2, CYP3A4, CYP3A5, DPYD, DRD2, F5, G6PD, GSTM1, GSTP1, HLA-B*1502, HLA-B*5701, HMGCR, IL28B, KCNH2, KCNJ11, MTHFR, NAT1, NAT2, NQO1, NR1I2, P2RY1, P2RY12, PTGIS, PTGS2, SCN5A, SLC15A2, SLC19A1, SLC22A1, SLC22A2, SLC22A6, SLCO1B1, SLCO1B3, SLCO2B1, SULT1A1, TPMT, TYMS, UGT1A1, UGT2B15, UGT2B7, VKORC1.

What about data security and privacy?

The security and privacy of personal genetic data are reasons for heavy concern among patients and medical professionals alike.

The MSC technology addresses concerns about data collection and analysis by third parties or governmental organizations, since no centralized collection and storage of personal genetic data is necessary. MSC data generation can be under the control of local healthcare institutions, insurances, labs or other qualified groups. Patients can be given full control over their genetic data, just as they have full control over the content of their wallets. Genetic data is never associated with the identity of a person in any database.

How can I generate Medicine Safety Codes myself?

We have created a web service where you can upload genetic data (in 23andMe or VCF format) to generate a Medicine Safety Code.

http://safety-code.org/generate.html

Please note that this service is still under development and should only be used for research purposes, never for guiding medical decision making.

Are special apps needed to interpret Medicine Safety Codes?

Medicine Safety Codes can be decoded with any common QR code reader app. In many cases, such apps come pre-installed on mobile devices. For example, many Android devices come pre-installed with an application called 'Google Goggles'. Simply activate the app and point the camera of your mobile phone towards the MSC QR code.

Some free QR code apps for Android:
Google Goggles
QR Droid

Free QR code apps for iOS:
QR Reader

If a standard QR reader is used, the anonymous genetic data is sent to the MSC server for decoding and interpreting. Alternatively, it is also possible to develop dedicated apps that can decode and interpret the data in the MSC themselves, without contacting an external service. Currently, we do not offer such dedicated apps, but plan on making them available soon.

Can pharmacogenomic data also be made available through other means, e.g., through electronic health records (EHRs)?

Yes it can. Our aim is to make essential, individual pharmacogenomic data as easily available as possible. While 2D barcodes are technically simple to implement, other technical solutions for providing data on essential pharmacogenes (such as EHRs) will also be addressed by the initiative.
